# The Landscape of Generative AI in Information Systems: A Synthesis of Secondary Reviews and Research Agendas

## 📄 Extended Abstract
**Context:** The post-ChatGPT surge has rapidly reframed research and practice in Information Systems (IS). As organizations and society grapple with the rapid adoption of GenAI, a body of secondary studies and research agendas has emerged to synthesize early evidence and chart directions for future inquiry.

**Objective:** This study conducts a systematic literature review of secondary studies and research agenda/roadmap papers to synthesize the current state of knowledge on the benefits and challenges of GenAI in IS and to identify proposed future research directions.

**Method:** We performed a systematic search across three major bibliographic databases (Scopus, Web of Science, and AIS eLibrary) for publications from 2023 onwards. Following a rigorous, multi-stage screening process, we selected a final set of 28 papers, comprising 18 secondary studies and 10 research agendas. Data were analyzed via bibliometric methods for landscape mapping and thematic analysis to synthesize key benefits, challenges, and emergent research agendas.
 
**Results:** Our analysis reveals an exceptionally swift scholarly response to the GenAI surge, marked by a concentrated wave of peer-reviewed secondary studies and agendas emerging in a remarkably short timeframe. The synthesized evidence highlights GenAI's transformative potential to drive productivity, accelerate innovation, personalize services, and democratize access to expertise. This potential, however, is tempered by multiple interrelated challenges, including technical unreliability (rooted in hallucinations and performance drift), pervasive societal and ethical risks (algorithmic bias, malicious misuse, skill erosion), and a systemic governance vacuum (privacy, accountability, and intellectual property). Finally, the synthesized research directions highlight the urgent need for inquiry into trustworthy human--AI interaction, organizational routines for hybrid teaming, and robust ethical and regulatory frameworks for responsible integration.

**Implications:** Our synthesis calls for reorienting IS scholarship from analyzing impacts toward actively shaping the co-evolution of GenAI capabilities with organizational routines, societal values, and regulatory institutions—emphasizing hybrid human-AI ensembles, situated validation, adaptive governance, and design principles for probabilistic systems. For practitioners and policymakers, our study serves as both a roadmap and a critical alert. Responsible GenAI adoption requires moving beyond technical implementation toward socio-technical co-design—balancing automation with human augmentation, establishing transparent governance structures, investing in AI literacy and ethical frameworks, and developing adaptive regulations that mitigate societal risks including labor disruption, bias amplification, and inequitable access while ensuring benefits are broadly shared.

**Conclusion:** The findings, interpreted through a socio-technical lens, reveal a persistent misalignment between the fast-evolving, powerful, yet flawed technical subsystem (GenAI) and the slower-adapting social subsystem (people, organizational structures, and institutional arrangements). The IS research community has a critical role in bridging this gap to achieve the joint optimization and harmony of GenAI capabilities and societal needs.
